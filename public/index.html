Hacker News highlights
No. 1 — harness fix · HN

I Improved 15 LLMs at Coding in One Afternoon. Only the Harness Changed

From the linkCan Boluk argues the evaluation harness, not the models, was the bottleneck in his coding benchmark. He compares patch, replace, and a new Hashline format that tags each line with a short hash, then runs 16 models across three edit tools. Hashline wins overall, cuts token usage, and turns one model’s score from about 7% to roughly 68%.

From the commentsHN readers echoed that edit harnesses dominate results and shared experiences building hash/AST‑based tooling. Some argued closed harnesses and subscriptions can hide real progress, while others focused on cost and tool ergonomics. The thread leaned toward open, standardized harnesses to make model comparisons fair.

No. 2 — peon pings · HN

Warcraft III Peon Voice Notifications for Claude Code

From the linkpeon-ping is a Claude Code hook that plays Warcraft III, StarCraft, or Red Alert voice lines when prompts, completions, permissions, or notifications fire. It ships with a CLI to enable categories, rotate packs, and tweak volume, plus a terminal‑tab updater. The README focuses on macOS but notes PowerShell support for WSL2.

From the commentsPeople loved the nostalgia and swapped favorite voice lines, with several proposing extra sound packs and theme ideas. Others shared lighter‑weight notification setups or questioned the complexity of hooks. A side conversation touched on Blizzard audio licensing and fair‑use boundaries.

No. 3 — arrow decade · HN

Apache Arrow is 10 years old

From the linkApache Arrow’s PMC celebrates the project’s 10‑year anniversary, pointing to the first commit in February 2016 and the push for a language‑agnostic columnar data format. The post notes the early 0.1.0 release, integration tests across implementations, and almost no breaking changes to the columnar/IPC formats since 1.0. It frames Arrow as the in‑memory counterpart to Parquet and highlights the growing ecosystem.

From the commentsCommenters emphasized Arrow’s role as a zero‑copy interchange layer that lets engines share data without serialization. Several clarified that it complements, rather than replaces, storage formats like Parquet or databases. Others reminisced about Feather/Pandas lineage and how Arrow unlocked tools like Polars and DuckDB.

No. 4 — engineering notebook · HN

Using an engineering notebook

From the linkNicole Tietz describes the engineering notebook as a practice, not a product: an original, dated, append‑only record of decisions, hypotheses, and experiments. She lists traits like real‑time updates and detail sufficient to reconstruct work later, and notes the habit improves memory and clarity. The post encourages trying it and tuning the ritual to your workflow.

From the commentsHN readers compared paper notebooks, daily logs, and digital systems like wikis or Obsidian. Some said paper is best for messy thinking and later synthesis, while others prefer searchable, shareable records. The consensus was that a notebook is optional but valuable for context and continuity.

No. 5 — zstd classifier · HN

Text classification with Python 3.14’s ZSTD module

From the linkPython 3.14’s new compression.zstd module enables a compact compression‑based text classifier. Halford builds a ZstdClassifier that keeps per‑label buffers and predicts the label that yields the smallest compressed size, then benchmarks on 20 Newsgroups. The approach hits about 91% accuracy in under two seconds, outperforming his older LZW variant and comparing well to TF‑IDF.

From the commentsDiscussion revolved around whether compression is a good proxy for semantics and how it stacks up against classic baselines. Others were excited to see Zstd in the stdlib and shared tips about dictionaries and reproducibility. The author chimed in that the main win is a simple, fast baseline enabled by the new API.

No. 6 — baloney kit · HN

Carl Sagan’s Baloney Detection Kit and the rules for critical thinking

From the linkOpen Culture revisits Carl Sagan’s baloney detection kit, listing nine critical‑thinking rules like independent confirmation, multiple hypotheses, disconfirmation, quantification, and falsifiability. It warns that the toolkit can be misused if applied mechanically. The piece ties the kit back to The Demon‑Haunted World and related Sagan talks.

From the commentsReaders debated how the kit works in a bad‑faith media environment and whether it needs a modern update. Others discussed Sagan’s famous “decline” quote and shared examples of using the kit on themselves. The thread stressed empathy and evidence as the core of skeptical inquiry.
