No. 1 — no-tip gauntlet · HN

Skip the Tips: A game to select "No Tip" but dark patterns try to stop you

From the linkSkip the Tips is a free browser game that turns tipping screens into a speedrun: you must hit “No Tip” while tiny buttons, guilt‑trip modals, fake loaders, and rigged sliders try to trick you. It frames itself as a satirical take on tipping culture, packs in 30+ dark patterns with progressive difficulty, and keeps the pressure up with a shrinking timer. Everything runs in-browser with no downloads or sign-ups.

From the commentsHN readers said the game nails the real engine of dark patterns: time pressure, social pressure, and cognitive load rather than just button placement. People compared their own tipping rules, called it a surprisingly solid reaction game, and shared rounds that felt extra devious or borderline unwinnable. The overall vibe was “this is awful in the best way.”

No. 2 — resize saga · HN

Resizing windows on macOS Tahoe – the saga continues

From the linkNorbert Heger followed up on Apple’s claim that macOS 26.3 fixed the window‑resize hit area. He wrote a test app that brute‑force clicks around the corner, showing the RC briefly aligned hit regions to the rounded corner but also thinned the resize band from 7 to 6 pixels. In the final release the fix vanished, returning to the old square regions and downgrading the issue to “Known Issue.”

From the commentsHN commenters argued that hit targets keep shrinking even as screens grow, and many want resize zones to be far larger along edges and corners. Others shared odd bugs like windows spanning two displays refusing to resize, or the resize cursor not appearing reliably. The thread mixes UI‑ergonomics frustration with a desire for more forgiving window controls.

No. 3 — retail commons · HN

Fixing retail with land value capture

From the linkThe essay argues that beloved retail districts create value that mostly accrues to nearby landowners, leaving shops to struggle. It surveys ways to capture and recycle that value, from unified ownership models like malls or rail‑plus‑property to hyperlocal institutions that tax land value and fund the “commons” of shops, cafes, and third spaces. The goal is to keep lively streets by letting retail share in the value it generates.

From the commentsHN readers dove into land value tax mechanics, pointing out land’s inelastic supply and how taxing appreciation could fund public goods or business relief. Others questioned feasibility, governance, and whether such schemes help renters versus landlords. The discussion was a mix of Georgist enthusiasm and implementation skepticism.

No. 4 — ai insult · HN

I was insulted today - AI style

From the linkThe author recounts proofreading a colleague’s report and suggesting a simple opening summary. The colleague praised the paragraph, then asked whether it was written with AI, which the author experienced as a deep insult to his craft. The post ends with a firm, humorous rebuttal and a broader frustration about the assumption that good writing must be machine‑generated.

From the commentsCommenters split between empathy and eye‑rolling, noting how AI has become a cultural identity marker rather than a tool debate. Some joked that “did you use AI?” is the modern insult, while others argued the simplest response is just “no.” The thread reads like a micro‑study in AI fatigue and social signaling.

No. 5 — $380b round · HN

Anthropic raises $30B in Series G funding at $380B post-money valuation

From the linkAnthropic says it raised $30B in a Series G led by GIC and Coatue, valuing the company at $380B post‑money and co‑led by D.E. Shaw, Dragoneer, Founders Fund, ICONIQ, and MGX. The announcement frames the capital as fuel for frontier research, product development, and infrastructure expansion, and highlights rapid enterprise adoption. It also cites a $14B run‑rate revenue and more than 500 customers spending over $1M annually.

From the commentsHN readers debated whether private mega‑valuations should face public‑company‑style scrutiny and how sustainable the numbers are. Many focused on revenue versus earnings and whether AI revenue growth justifies the price tag. Others pointed to Claude Code’s reported growth as evidence of real traction, while skeptics compared the valuation to public market benchmarks.

No. 6 — mobile fortify · HN

ICE, CBP Knew Facial Recognition App Couldn’t Do What DHS Says It Could, Deployed It Anyway

From the linkTechdirt reports that ICE and CBP have been using NEC’s “Mobile Fortify” app to identify people even though required privacy and AI impact assessments lagged behind deployment. The piece argues DHS overstates the app’s identity‑verification capabilities, noting it doesn’t actually verify identities and has been used on people beyond targeted suspects. It frames the rollout as “deploy first, paperwork later,” with civil‑liberties risks baked in.

From the commentsHN commenters questioned whether the app is being mischaracterized, arguing it’s more of a similarity search that leaves the final call to officers. Others pushed for real accuracy/false‑positive data and highlighted the privacy implications of using imperfect face matching at scale. The thread mixes technical nitpicks with concern over surveillance and due process.
