No. 1 — email tempo · HN

Do not apologize for replying late to my email

From the linkPloum argues that email is asynchronous by design, so late replies don’t need apology or justification unless there was an explicit deadline. The post encourages shorter, low-pressure replies (or no reply at all), avoids “I’ll reply later” messages, and suggests keeping context in-thread with bottom-posting and trimmed quotes. The goal is to reduce guilt and cognitive load while preserving clarity for both sides.

From the commentsHN readers split on etiquette and expectations: some treat a 24–48 hour reply window as professional courtesy, while others say email is low-priority compared to tickets, chat, or calls. Several noted cultural norms matter, and that visible expectations change the tone. Others saw apologizing as a harmless social convention, while a few argued it can create entitlement or unnecessary pressure.

No. 2 — simcity + agents · HN

Show HN: AI agents play SimCity through a REST API

From the linkHallucinating Splines is a public, live city simulator where AI agents act as mayors. The site lists cities, populations, scores, and a leaderboard, and invites visitors to “start building.” It notes the simulation is based on Micropolis (open-sourced SimCity) and built on micropolisJS, with public cities and a persistent, running simulation.

From the commentsThe HN thread is marked “discuss” and has no community comments yet, so there isn’t feedback to summarize at this time.

No. 3 — extension spying · HN

Chrome extensions spying on 37M users' browsing data

From the linkThe author describes an automated pipeline that runs Chrome in Docker, routes traffic through a MITM proxy, and flags extensions whose outbound requests correlate with synthetic URLs. Using that leakage metric, they identified 287 extensions that exfiltrate browsing history, totaling about 37.4 million installs. The post frames the threat model around profiling/ads, corporate URL leakage, and the risk of pairing history with cookies.

From the commentsDiscussion focused on extension supply-chain risk and how innocuous add-ons can be sold and weaponized later. Commenters debated how much auditing is realistic, with some calling for provenance or trusted publishing and others noting we rely on institutions to police the ecosystem. A few threads argued that “don’t trust Google” isn’t sufficient; the issue is the broader incentive structure and enforcement.

No. 4 — telnet blackout · HN

The Day the Telnet Died

From the linkGreyNoise reports a sharp, sustained drop in global telnet traffic on January 14, 2026, describing a step change from roughly 74k sessions to 22k in an hour, with multiple ASNs and countries going silent. The post notes the timing relative to CVE-2026-24061 and frames the event as an infrastructure-level shift rather than a gradual decline in scanning.

From the commentsReaders debated whether ISP-level filtering of port 23 is prudent security or an overreach that violates net-neutrality principles. Some pointed out that legacy apps like MUDs still use telnet and would be harmed by blanket blocks, while others argued that privileged ports should be reserved for their intended protocols. The thread mixes nostalgia with practical security tradeoffs.

No. 5 — jwst + z14 · HN

A Cosmic Miracle: A Remarkably Luminous Galaxy at z=14.44 Confirmed with JWST

From the linkThe Open Journal of Astrophysics paper reports JWST/NIRSpec confirmation of a very luminous galaxy, MoM-z14, at z=14.44 (about 280 million years after the Big Bang). The authors cite a clear Lyman-α break and UV emission lines, arguing that bright galaxies at z≈14–15 are more common than expected. They interpret this as evidence for rapidly rising star formation and compact, low-dust systems in the early universe.

From the commentsCommenters corrected a recombination-time mix-up, noted the unusually long author list, and shared an arXiv link because the journal page was heavy to load. A few threads veered into reionization and radio/optical observation basics, using the paper as a springboard for cosmology context.

No. 6 — self-hosted git · HN

End of an era for me: no more self-hosted git

From the linkGerd Hoffmann announces he’s shutting down a self-hosted git/cvs server that’s been public since 2011 after AI scrapers overwhelmed the cgit frontend with nonstop requests. Rather than rebuild the server or fight the scraping, he’s keeping mirrors on GitHub and GitLab as the primary repos. The post frames the decision as a reluctant but practical end to an era.

From the commentsHN feedback ranged from tactical mitigations (Crowdsec, blocking suspicious 4xx patterns) to a broader debate about scraping incentives. Commenters argued many scrapers are harvesting data for AI training, with weak enforcement and a “wild west” of third-party collectors. Others noted the rise of residential proxies and the difficulty of defending small self-hosted sites.
