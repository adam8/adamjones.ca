No. 1 — trust + gatekeeping · HN

Vouch: a community trust management system

From the linkVouch proposes a lightweight, file-backed system to gate contributor access: only vouched users can open issues, PRs, or discussions. It includes workflows for asking for vouches, denouncing bad actors, and plugging into GitHub automation, framing “trust” as a deliberate, community-owned input to tooling.

From the commentsHN discussion circles around friction versus openness. Some see it as a reasonable trust gate for small teams, while others prefer alternative filters (sponsorship, payments, or moderation queues). A recurring theme is keeping incentives healthy: people want a way to reduce spam without making contribution feel paywalled.

No. 2 — remembrance + networks · HN

Dave Farber has died

From the linkThe NANOG list memorial shares that David J. Farber passed away on February 7, 2026 in Tokyo at age 91, and reflects on his impact as a teacher, mentor, and pioneer in networking and computing.

From the commentsHN responses are personal and historical: commenters trade memories, link to his biography, and highlight the systems he helped build (CSNET, NSFNET, gigabit testbeds). The tone is appreciative and archival, with many emphasizing how much of today’s Internet traces to his influence.

No. 3 — red planet + colonies · HN

Show HN: Underhill, a Mars colony RPG inspired by the Mars trilogy

From the linkUnderhill is a Mars colony RPG that mixes survival, exploration, and settlement-building, explicitly inspired by Kim Stanley Robinson’s Mars novels. The page pitches it as a story-forward sim where you recruit colonists, build infrastructure, and adapt to the planet’s harsh constraints.

From the commentsEarly players note UI friction and balance issues (pop-up flow, placement, costs), then follow up with concrete suggestions on pacing and feedback. The creator jumps in to acknowledge bugs and tweaks, and the overall thread reads like a live playtest with rapid iteration.

No. 4 — local-first + memory · HN

Show HN: LocalGPT, a local-first AI assistant in Rust

From the linkLocalGPT is a Rust-based assistant built around local persistence: a markdown-backed memory store, optional client UIs, and pluggable model providers. It positions itself as a single binary that can talk to local endpoints or remote APIs while keeping data on your machine.

From the commentsCommenters debate what “local-first” really means if inference still happens in the cloud. The consensus leans toward separating data residency from model hosting, with requests for bundled local models or clearer defaults for running on-device.

No. 5 — legacy code + booleans · HN

The little bool of doom

From the linkThis post traces a Fedora mass-rebuild failure for chocolate-doom when C23 makes `false` a keyword. A quick `_Bool` swap revealed that legacy code stored non-boolean values in “boolean” fields, leading to weird behavior when `255` is treated as both true and false under the standard.

From the commentsHN discussion debates how much compatibility old C code should expect across standards and compilers. Some emphasize fixing the undefined behavior, while others note practical build constraints and the complexity of untangling decades-old assumptions.

No. 6 — agents + craft · HN

OpenClaw is changing my life

From the linkThe post reflects on how OpenClaw shifts the developer role toward directing and supervising agents rather than hand-writing every line. It argues that the value is still in intentionality: clear specs, quick verification loops, and deciding where automation should stop.

From the commentsCommenters echo the need for human oversight and tight feedback loops. Several point out that “agentic” wins come from strong task framing and reviews, not from relinquishing control, and that the best workflows still look like human-led craft.
