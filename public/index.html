No. 1 · HN

News publishers curb Internet Archive access amid AI scraping concerns

From linkNieman Lab reports that major publishers are tightening access to the Internet Archive because they fear AI companies are using the Wayback Machine and its APIs as a backdoor to scrape content. The article highlights The Guardian and The New York Times blocking archive.org bots in robots.txt and explains how a broader review of publishers’ robots files found 241 outlets explicitly disallowing at least one Internet Archive crawler, with many of those blocks coming from the USA Today/Gannett network.

From commentsHN readers argued over the role of independent archives: some said accepting coverage means accepting third-party preservation, while others defended time-delayed archives or paywall-friendly controls so publishers can still fund reporting. A parallel thread pushed back on the idea of “trustworthy” sources as a single axis, noting bias is inevitable and readers must weigh context.

No. 2 · HN

You can’t trust the internet anymore

From linkNicole’s short rant starts with a niche gaming discovery (a Genesis cartridge that is secretly a Master System game) and turns into a broader lament about search results and the web’s declining reliability. A mix of SEO sludge, misinformation, and low-effort sites keeps bubbling to the top, making it hard to trust what you find even when you’re researching obscure but legitimate topics.

From commentsCommenters debated how to rebuild trust: one camp argued for local or private networks to avoid AI-driven scraping and content rot, while others said the problem is the content itself and can be solved with smaller trusted communities without rebuilding the network stack. Several replies cited AI agents and scraping traffic as a force multiplier for the decline.

No. 3 · HN

Gemini 3 Deep Think drew a convincing pelican-on-a-bicycle SVG

From linkSimon Willison shares a Gemini 3 Deep Think experiment where the model generates a detailed SVG of a pelican riding a bicycle and then tackles a tougher prompt describing a California brown pelican with specific anatomical features. The post contrasts these results with prior models and links to the generated SVGs as evidence of improved vector-graphics output.

From commentsHN discussion splits between people convinced the demo is legitimate progress and skeptics who think the best results are cherry-picked; several commenters report trying their own SVG prompts across models and seeing wide variance. Others note that comparing different assistants makes the pace of improvement feel uneven and hard to judge.

No. 4 · HN

Vim 9.2 ships with Vim9, diff, and UI upgrades

From linkThe Vim 9.2 release highlights major improvements to Vim9 script (enums, tuples, and generics), richer completion behavior, and significant diff-mode enhancements like linematch and inline highlighting. It also brings platform updates such as Wayland support, XDG-based config paths, new UI tweaks like a vertical tabpanel, and an updated interactive tutor.

From commentsHN commenters compared Vim’s trajectory with Neovim’s Lua-heavy ecosystem, asking whether more plugin authors will adopt Vim9script. Some noted they now prefer Vim9script for plugins, while others said Lua in Neovim was the reason they switched editors in the first place.

No. 5 · HN

Smart sleep mask exposes EEG data over an open MQTT broker

From linkThe author reverse-engineers a Kickstarter smart sleep mask and discovers a shared MQTT broker with hardcoded credentials inside the Flutter app binary. By inspecting BLE traffic, decompiling the APK, and extracting string tables, they reconstruct the protocol shape and build a simple web control panel to send commands and read streamed EEG data.

From commentsReaders used the story as a cautionary tale about hardware startups taking shortcuts: LLMs can make teams believe firmware and software are “free,” but the hard parts still show up later. Others emphasized the privacy risk of shared broker credentials and the broader IoT habit of shipping without secure local-only modes.

No. 6 · HN

Show HN: Sameshi, a ~1200 Elo chess engine in 2KB

From linkSameshi is a micro chess engine implemented in roughly 2KB, using a 120-cell mailbox board, negamax with alpha-beta pruning, material-only evaluation, and capture-first move ordering. It supports legal move validation for check, mate, and stalemate, reports an estimated ~1170 Elo from Stockfish tests, and explicitly omits castling, en passant, promotion, and repetition rules to stay tiny.

From commentsHN commenters praised the demoscene-style minimalism but questioned how much space the missing rules would cost, arguing that castling, en passant, and promotion are essential for “real” chess. Others compared it with older tiny engines and discussed tradeoffs between size, completeness, and playing strength.
