No. 1 · HN

/e/OS

From linkThe /e/OS site pitches the project as a whole de-Googled mobile stack rather than just another Android fork. It combines a privacy-hardened Android base with Murena Workspace, email, cloud storage, office tools, and app compatibility layers so the user can leave Google services without giving up a functioning daily-driver phone. The emphasis is practical independence: keep mainstream phone ergonomics, keep most Android apps, but swap in a service layer that exposes trackers, rates privacy behavior, and gives the user a more explicit handle on what the phone is sending out.

From commentsHN commenters treated the project as a realistic compromise rather than a perfect privacy story. Supporters liked that /e/OS tries to meet ordinary users where they are by preserving app compatibility and lowering the friction of leaving Google, while skeptics questioned whether any Android-derived stack can sustainably keep pace with upstream security, hardware quirks, and the sheer maintenance cost of mobile platforms. The thread kept returning to the same tradeoff: strict purity is rare, so the real question is whether a privacy-focused fork can stay usable enough to matter.

No. 2 · HN

What is a fast page?

From linkThe article argues that “fast” should be understood from the user’s point of view instead of as a single benchmark score. A fast page shows something useful quickly, keeps the network and rendering pipeline proportional to the amount of content being delivered, and reveals structure in an order that matches human attention rather than framework convenience. Its larger point is that speed is mostly a discipline problem: smaller documents, fewer round trips, clearer hierarchy, and less speculative work often matter more than post-hoc optimization theater.

From commentsHN readers turned the thread into a debate over how much performance should be measured by tools versus felt by humans. Some liked the article’s attempt to move beyond Lighthouse score-chasing and back toward perceived responsiveness, while others pushed for sharper definitions because “fast” gets abused as an aesthetic claim. A side conversation spun out around JavaScript-heavy sites, native-like expectations, and whether modern tooling quietly normalizes shipping too much code, but the common thread was that good performance starts with restraint long before it becomes a tuning exercise.

No. 3 · HN

Making Video Games in 2025 (Without an Engine)

From linkNoel Berry’s post is a detailed case for building a commercial-quality game with a tiny self-owned stack instead of a general-purpose engine. Using C, SDL, custom data formats, a homemade renderer, platform builds, and CI across macOS, Linux, and Windows, the piece frames “no engine” work as both a technical preference and a way to keep every moving part legible. The attraction is not purity for its own sake but control: fewer black boxes, tighter feedback loops, and a toolchain shaped around one game’s constraints rather than around the assumptions of a massive editor.

From commentsHN readers respected the engineering discipline but immediately split on whether the economics make sense outside a certain kind of developer. Fans of the post saw it as proof that understanding and owning the stack can still be productive, especially for people who enjoy systems work and want to avoid engine churn. More skeptical commenters asked where custom tooling stops being empowering and starts stealing time from the actual game, often comparing the tradeoffs to using Godot, Unity, or Unreal when shipping speed, content iteration, and existing ecosystem support matter more than maximal control.

No. 4 · HN

Motorola Announces Partnership with GrapheneOS Foundation

From linkThe GrapheneOS announcement says Motorola and the GrapheneOS Foundation are working together to bring the security-focused Android distribution to a mainstream hardware partner, starting with the Motorola Edge 60 Pro. Even though the linked post is short, the significance is obvious: GrapheneOS has mostly lived in a niche of Pixel owners willing to optimize for security above convenience, and this partnership hints at a broader path where a major OEM treats alternate secure software as a supported option instead of an enthusiast side channel.

From commentsHN commenters were interested but careful. Many saw the deal as validation that GrapheneOS has outgrown hobby-project status and could make hardened Android easier to buy into, especially for enterprise or privacy-sensitive users who do not want to hunt for one blessed phone line. At the same time, readers pushed on the usual failure points for Android hardware promises: carrier variants, unlockability, firmware update quality, regional support, and whether a close OEM relationship might eventually soften some of the project’s hard-line stance in exchange for convenience.

No. 5 · HN

Jolla phone: a full-stack European alternative to mainstream smartphones

From linkJolla’s page sells the C2 Community Phone less as a spec-sheet monster and more as a statement that the full mobile stack can still be owned differently. The pitch centers on Sailfish OS, European service independence, a privacy mode hardware switch, community-minded distribution, and a phone experience that tries to stay outside the standard iOS-versus-Android channel. It reads like an argument for sovereignty and controllability over mainstream polish: the value is not winning benchmarks, but proving that phones can still be treated as personal computing devices rather than fixed ecosystems.

From commentsHN readers responded with equal parts nostalgia and skepticism. People who miss the era of mobile experimentation liked seeing another attempt at an alternative stack and were especially interested in the privacy controls and European positioning, but the hard questions arrived quickly around app compatibility, update cadence, security maintenance, and whether tiny mobile ecosystems can attract enough users to survive. The thread did not reject the idea of a different phone future, but it repeatedly asked whether community energy alone can outrun the brutal economics that have flattened most previous attempts.

No. 6 · HN

Introducing WebMCP: a browser extension for AI agents

From linkWebMCP presents itself as a lightweight bridge between a real browser session and any MCP-capable agent. Instead of rebuilding login state in a separate automation rig, the extension exposes the current browser context, tabs, screenshots, DOM interactions, and scriptable actions through a standard tool interface, which makes “use the browser I already have open” a first-class agent capability. The interesting part is not just convenience; it is the attempt to standardize browser control as ordinary tool use so agents can operate against live authenticated pages without bespoke glue for every workflow.

From commentsHN commenters were enthusiastic about the ergonomics but cautious about the security envelope. People liked the idea of reusing an already-authenticated browser and avoiding one-off Playwright stacks for every task, and several readers immediately compared the project to Browser Use, Stagehand, and other browser-agent tools. The harder discussion was about boundaries: how much power an extension should hand to an agent, how tab control and credential exposure should be scoped, and whether MCP is becoming a durable interoperability layer or just another transient abstraction that still needs stronger conventions.
